1 · Context

A blood test for transformers

Power transformers are critical, million-euro assets. When something goes wrong inside — overheating, sparking, arcing — the insulating oil breaks down and releases dissolved gases. Measuring them is called DGA: Dissolved Gas Analysis.

Each gas is a clue:

  • H2 — electrical sparking (early warning)
  • C2H2 (acetylene) — arcing, the most dangerous fault
  • C2H4 / CH4 / C2H6 — overheating, by temperature
  • CO / CO2 — the paper insulation is degrading

The problem: experts are scarce, labels don't exist

The Duval Triangle — an expert rule from the 1970s, still the industry standard.
  • Expert rules (Duval, IEC ratios) work — but need an expert to apply, and often answer “undetermined”.
  • Machine learning reaches 99% accuracy in papers — but needs labelled faults, which utilities almost never have.
  • Our fleet: zero confirmed fault labels. Just gas numbers and messy operator notes.

Goal: rank 628 transformers by risk and read fault types — using no labels at all.

What “real” means here

  • Gas values stored as text, with dashes for missing
  • Operator notes mix English and Thai — 69% contain Thai
  • Notes log everything: real faults, but mostly maintenance and admin
  • No ground truth anywhere — the data was assembled from multiple operational sources and anonymised

Why that is interesting

Most published methods are tested on small, clean, balanced datasets (often <200 samples). Whether they survive contact with a real fleet is exactly the open question — and, as we will see, the messiness itself hides a scientific finding.

2 · Contribution 1 — fault types without labels

The severity trap

Feed raw gas concentrations to any model, and it learns “how much gas” — not “which fault”. The same fault at low and high gassing looks like two different things.

For the experts A plain autoencoder on log-concentrations encodes magnitude: total gas is linearly readable from its 2-D latent (R² = 0.63), and agreement with Duval classes is near-noise (ARI 0.14–0.16).

2 · Contribution 1 — fault types without labels

Use the right geometry, and fault types appear

Agreement with the expert (Duval) classes, from raw inputs to our representation.
0.16 → 0.55
agreement with expert diagnosis (ARI) — 3.4× better, with zero labels

The trick is not a bigger model. It is treating gas proportions with the mathematics made for proportions (log-ratios), then using the simplest possible projection.

For the experts Centred log-ratio (Aitchison geometry) on the 5 combustible gases → standardise → PCA-2 → KMeans (k=7). ARI 0.545 ± 0.002 vs Duval. Deterministic. Holds on a strict temporal split: fit pre-2022, score 2022+ → ARI 0.45.

A data-driven diagnostic map — and an honest negative

Every dot is an oil sample. Colours = expert diagnosis, added afterwards — the map itself never saw them.
  • Discharge, partial-discharge and thermal regions emerge on their own
  • Readable axes — an engineer can interpret the directions
  • Deterministic: same data in, same map out

negative result

We also built a deep autoencoder (SD-CAE) and an adversarial variant. Neither beat the simple linear map. We report both as negatives in the paper — knowing what does not work is a result too.

3 · Contribution 2 — the validation trap

“The more you look, the more you find”

To check our risk score, we used the only “truth” available: operator-logged field events. Then we noticed something wrong.

For the experts This is surveillance / informed-presence bias, well known in medicine and electronic health records (Sackett 1979; Haut & Pronovost 2011; Goldstein 2016). To our knowledge, never reported for DGA — models in this field are routinely validated against such labels.
CESI × KMUTNB — Research Initiation 202610
3 · Contribution 2 — the validation trap

Three tests, one verdict

(a) The tie. Simply counting how often a unit was sampled predicts logged events as well as all our chemistry combined (0.76 vs 0.74 — statistically indistinguishable).

(b) No foresight. Control for the sample count, and the score's ability to predict future events drops to a coin flip (0.50).

(c) The fix. Define the target by chemistry (arcing onset) instead of attention — now gases predict (0.64) and the count does not (0.49).

Example finds

“ตามผลเนื่องจาก Bkr. ระเบิด” — follow-up after breaker explosion

The punchline

On the repaired label, the sample-count's advantage disappears entirely (0.734 vs 0.735). Part of the “trap” was simply that the label was incomplete — in a language the pipeline didn't read.

5 · Take-aways

What this project says to the field

1

The right geometry beats a bigger model.

A linear method in the correct mathematical space outperformed deep networks — simpler, interpretable, deployable.

2

Validation labels can lie.

Any DGA risk model validated on operator-logged events should report the sample-count “confound floor” — otherwise surveillance can be mistaken for diagnostic skill.

3

Honest negatives are results.

Deep SVDD, VAE, adversarial disentanglement, RUL modelling: tested, rejected, documented. Future work starts further ahead because of it.

Deliverables: a 6-page IEEE paper draft (3 contributions) · a reproducible pipeline (one command regenerates every number, 10 automated tests) · an anonymised public dataset extract · the interactive dashboard.

5 · Take-aways

What five weeks of research taught me

  • The scientific method, for real — hypothesis, test, and the discipline to pivot when the evidence disagrees with the plan
  • Statistical honesty — confidence intervals, paired tests, blocked bootstraps; “0.76 vs 0.74” means nothing until you test it
  • Attack your own results first — the finding that a simple method beat our own headline model came from auditing ourselves before reviewers would
  • A full research stack — Python/PyTorch, statistics, LaTeX/IEEE writing, git, automated testing, Streamlit
  • Working across languages and cultures — a French student, a Thai fleet, English science; 211 Thai field notes read and classified
  • Scoping — five weeks forces choices: what to cut, what to consolidate, what to hand to future work
